People, Places, Patterns, Problems

Section titled “People, Places, Patterns, Problems”

A foundation for crime analysis and progressive policing.

Policing models can sometimes cause confusion between agencies:

  • Many are layered on top of each other.
  • No single model works for every department.
  • Examples: POP, COPS, EBP, ILP, HSP, Smart Policing, Predictive Policing, CompStat, DDACTS.

The Four Ps

Section titled “The Four Ps”

Recurring crime may call for proactive policing.

  1. People (Intel): Offenders working together.
    • Traditional investigations, Internal Affairs, Pulling Levers, ILP
  2. Places (Strategic): High-activity locations.
    • Hot Spots, HSP, DDACTS, “Broken Windows”
  3. Patterns (Tactical): Series of offenses with shared traits.
    • Tactical analysis, Routine Activity Theory
  4. Problems (Strategic): Chronic issues, not always tied to one offender.
    • POP, COPS, CPTED

Agencies must address all Four Ps.
Patterns touch nearly every department function.
Problems require long-term, prevention-oriented solutions.

What Do We Need?

Section titled “What Do We Need?”
  • Every precinct and unit must focus on proactive crime reduction.
  • Prioritize actionable analysis—charts alone aren’t enough.
  • Know what strategies and tactics work.
  • Invest in professional development for both analysts and officers.
  • Develop a strategic plan tailored to your agency.

A crime analysis product doesn’t need to look good—it needs to drive action.

A Synthesis Model

Section titled “A Synthesis Model”

Use a blended model that:

  • Focuses on current crime patterns.
  • Monitors chronic and long-term problems.
  • Combines enforcement and deterrence for recurring issues.
  • Makes use of uncommitted patrol time to manage hotspots.

For each issue:

  • Use analysis to predict future activity.
  • Choose effective tactics—not just convenient ones.
  • Meet regularly to evaluate and adjust strategies.
  • Communicate outcomes to the community and incorporate feedback.

Strive for prevention—don’t assume crime is inevitable.

Organizational Placement & Analyst Impact

Section titled “Organizational Placement & Analyst Impact”

Analysts are most effective when close to leadership (e.g., Chief or Deputy Chief).

If analysis is being ignored:

  • Are the products actionable?
  • If yes, is the issue rapport or delivery?

Analysts may need:

  • More face time with officers.
  • To attend briefings, go on ride-alongs, or visit crime scenes.

Conclusion

Section titled “Conclusion”

The “Four Ps” framework—People, Places, Patterns, and Problems—offers a strategic lens for building a proactive, effective crime analysis program. Rather than relying on a single policing model, agencies benefit most when they tailor approaches, analyze recurring issues, and deploy tactics rooted in evidence.

Success lies not in complexity, but in clarity, consistency, and communication:

  • Align analysis with daily operations.
  • Focus on actionable intelligence, not just information.
  • Invest in people and professional development to support long-term problem-solving.
  • Ensure that analysts are empowered—not isolated—and that their work connects to decision-makers and patrol operations.

Ultimately, crime prevention becomes possible when agencies move from reaction to intention, and from data collection to action. The Four Ps provide that roadmap.
