12 Questions Executives Should Ask

Outline of twelve critical questions for executive leaders to assess and enhance their crime analysis capabilities, covering topics like staffing, data access, analyst placement, actionable reporting, professional development, and organizational integration.

Link to the original webinar on Vimeo:

12 Questions Executives Should Ask

This webinar assumes the person listening holds an Executive- or Command-level role with the authority to make changes to how business is done.

Analysts might get stuck in a rut when not asked or challenged.

What Crime Analysis Does

Section titled “What Crime Analysis Does”
  • Solve crimes
  • Develop effective tactics and strategies
  • Find and apprehend offenders
  • Improve safety and quality of life
  • Optimize internal operations
  • Prioritize patrol and investigation
  • Detect and solve community problems
  • Enact effective policies
  • Educate the public

1. Is my analysis comprehensive?

Section titled “1. Is my analysis comprehensive?”

Regardless of your policing model, you must be proactive and tackle crime in groups. Analysts often use the “4 Ps” to structure their work:

  • People – Offenders, victims, or persons of interest
  • Places – High-crime areas or “hot spots”
  • Patterns – Trends or repeat criminal methods
  • Problems – Root causes, often approached via SARA (Scanning, Analysis, Response, Assessment)

2. Do we have enough people?

Section titled “2. Do we have enough people?”

Refer to the “Effectively plan, resource, and administer” section of the Get the Most From Crime Analysis document.

You can supplement analysts from:

  • Sworn personnel with time and skills
  • Analysts from other agencies
  • Interns or volunteers
  • Grant-funded positions

3. Are they (Crime Analysts) located in the right place?

Section titled “3. Are they (Crime Analysts) located in the right place?”

Analysts should ideally report to the (Assistant) Chief of Police, above the level of precinct or task force division.

They do not supervise others, but serve the entire agency.

4. Are they allocated the best way?

Section titled “4. Are they allocated the best way?”

If you have only one analyst but multiple locations, rotate the analyst periodically.

Three strategies for allocation:

1. Geographic Area

Section titled “1. Geographic Area”
  • Assigned by location
  • Area-based command
  • Centralized oversight

2. Crime Type

Section titled “2. Crime Type”
  • Dedicated to specific crime types
  • May be driven by grant requirements
  • Ideal if patterns justify specialization

3. Analysis Type

Section titled “3. Analysis Type”
  • Tactical or Strategic
  • Rotating assignments expands skill diversity
  • Daily scans are essential

In larger agencies:

Strategic Analysis
Section titled “Strategic Analysis”
  • Focus: Long-term planning
  • Use: Policy, prevention strategy
  • Audience: Executives
Tactical Analysis
Section titled “Tactical Analysis”
  • Focus: Short-term support
  • Use: Crime series, networks
  • Audience: Investigations, mid-level command
Operational Analysis (aka Intelligence)
Section titled “Operational Analysis (aka Intelligence)”
  • Focus: Immediate response
  • Use: Deployment, briefings
  • Audience: Patrol, incident command

5. Do analysts have access to the data they need?

Section titled “5. Do analysts have access to the data they need?”

Ask your analysts directly: What’s blocking you?

They need direct access, ideally via ODBC:

  • Avoids RMS limitations
  • Enables queries, filters, maps, analysis

Common data problems:

  • Completeness – Missing data
  • Validity – Incorrect formats
  • Accuracy – Mistakes
  • Timeliness – Delays

Policies must support valid, accurate, timely data collection.

6. Do analysts have rapport with officers?

Section titled “6. Do analysts have rapport with officers?”

Trust ensures their work supports real operations.

Hurts rapport:

  • Physical separation
  • Mandatory request systems
  • Limited contact
  • Lack of role understanding
  • Using analysis as discipline
  • Useless or ignored reports

Builds rapport:

  • Ride-alongs
  • Briefing attendance
  • Central location
  • Event participation
  • Exec endorsement
  • Actionable content

7. Are the analysts’ products actionable?

Section titled “7. Are the analysts’ products actionable?”

Charts and numbers aren’t enough.

Effective products:

  • Use the 5WH framework
  • Include quality checks
  • Combine police and non-police data
  • Answer strategic and operational questions
  • Make predictions
  • Identify action points
  • Recommend responses

8. Are the analysis products used?

Section titled “8. Are the analysis products used?”

People

Section titled “People”
  • Intelligence collection
  • UC operations
  • Priority prosecution
  • Warrant service
  • Parole revocation
  • Focused deterrence

Places

Section titled “Places”
  • Hot spot policing (HSP)
  • Foot/bike patrols
  • DDACTS
  • CPTED
  • Place management
  • Closures/seizures

Patterns

Section titled “Patterns”
  • Rapid tactical response

Problems

Section titled “Problems”
  • Problem-oriented policing (POP)
  • Situational crime prevention

Ask yourself:

  • Are analysts embedded in decision-making?
  • Are their products guiding patrol?
  • Do investigators rely on them?
  • Are tactics shaped by analysis?
  • Are prevention units engaging based on findings?

9. Do analysts have a professional development plan?

Section titled “9. Do analysts have a professional development plan?”

Watch from 01h 01m 03s for training examples.

  • Do they have personal goals?
  • Without new input, analysts stagnate
  • IACA offers excellent professional development

10. Do my analysts participate in professional associations?

Section titled “10. Do my analysts participate in professional associations?”

Encourage participation in:

  • IACA – International Association of Crime Analysts
  • IALEIA – International Association of Law Enforcement Intelligence Analysts

These promote:

  • Standards
  • Training
  • Networking
  • Field-wide innovation

11. Am I clear in my requests?

Section titled “11. Am I clear in my requests?”

Unclear or vague requests waste time and frustrate staff.

Be specific. Ask operational questions. Define your scope.

12. Would the outputs of my crime analysis unit hold up to scrutiny?

Section titled “12. Would the outputs of my crime analysis unit hold up to scrutiny?”

Anticipate accountability.

  • Is the data solid?
  • Are methods sound?
  • Are findings reasonable and verified?
  • Are definitions and categories clear?
  • Would this stand up to public or media review?

Weak analysis reflects on leadership—demand excellence.

Signs of a Good Crime Analysis Capability

Section titled “Signs of a Good Crime Analysis Capability”
  • Actionable, regular reports
  • Enough analysts for the workload
  • Logical placement in org structure
  • Smart allocation
  • Direct data access
  • Officer rapport
  • Clear, accurate findings
  • Professional growth pathways

  • Monitor data quality
  • Resolve duplicates (e.g., cultural naming conventions such as patronym/matronym)

Conclusion

Section titled “Conclusion”

Strong crime analysis capabilities are not a luxury—they are essential for effective, modern policing. These twelve questions help executive leaders assess whether their agency is positioned to turn data into decisions and analysis into action.

An empowered crime analysis unit—properly staffed, strategically placed, and professionally supported—can drive operational efficiency, inform policy, support officers, and ultimately improve public safety. But this requires intentional leadership: ensuring analysts have access to quality data, a seat at the table, clear direction, and opportunities to grow.

Crime trends evolve. So must the agencies that seek to address them. Leaders who regularly evaluate and invest in their analytical capabilities will be best positioned to protect their communities today—and shape the future of policing tomorrow.
