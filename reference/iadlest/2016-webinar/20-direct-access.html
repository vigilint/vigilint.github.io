The Benefits of Direct Data Access

Data-driven strategies are transforming law enforcement by enabling smarter, faster, and more equitable decision-making. This presentation explores how direct access to quality data empowers analysts and officers to identify patterns, improve operations, and reduce crime effectively.

Part 1: What is Data-Driven Analysis?

Section titled “Part 1: What is Data-Driven Analysis?”

Continue watching at 11m 01s.

Modern approaches (from BJA, CompStat, DDACTS, IACA, IACP, etc.) to Data-Driven Analysis have been used to reduce jail populations, stabilize individuals & families, and save money in the process.

ChatGPT Response

Simply hiring more officers and chasing crime did not address root causes. Modern data-driven approaches, when done well, focus on prevention, efficiency, equity, and outcomes—leading to safer communities and fewer people in jail or prison.

  • Reactive policing: Crime analysts were underutilized. The focus was on responding to crimes after they happened, not on prediction or prevention.
  • Evidence-based practices: Programs like LEAD (Law Enforcement Assisted Diversion), pre-arrest diversion, and community-based supervision have been guided by crime analysis and shown to reduce reoffending and jail use.

Data-Driven Strategies provide direct access to records for analysis to forecast where and when past, present, and future crimes occur; Crime Analysis prefaces operations.

  • These also merge data sets to reduce crime, and improve intervention and response effectiveness.

Most crime is random, unpredictable, and unconnected.

Some crime is:

  • Not isolated (targeted)
  • Emergent patterns (predictable)
  • Specific targets (connected)

Hot Spot Policing is not helpful information without providing the context (person, place, time).

Calls For Service (CFS)

Section titled “Calls For Service (CFS)”

Having hundreds of thousands of calls each year alone (as a chart) is not helpful enough for a Crime Analyst to enact policy change or form an operation to reduce. We need more information!

  • What are the Call trends?
  • What are officers spending time doing?
  • Is the department more reactive (from call to call) or proactive (crime prevention)?
  • Are there repeat offenders? Locations, hotspots, predictions?
  • Does enforcement align with the hotspots?

Part 2: Benefits of Data-Driven Strategies

Section titled “Part 2: Benefits of Data-Driven Strategies”

Tactical Old School: Reactive policing sends an army of officers in response (saturation).

  • Disjointed databases & paper documentation is the old way.

New Data-Driven Strategies

  • Identify patterns and trends as they emerge
    • Discovering trends is easier when associated data is accessible (rather than needing to go searching for links)
    • I think we’re overcoming the era of doing things manually.
  • Identify investigative leads
    • Connected databases across state lines enable strong link analysis
    • Predict where and when a suspect continues a pattern
    • List of potential suspects (probation/parolee)
    • Assist in Link Analysis
  • Automated reporting
    • Program Queries: Setup alert when certain crimes outside a threshold
  • Develop deployment strategies
    • Hot Spots lead to trends (month, season, time, etc.)
  • Identify and repair data quality issues
    • Unclear which addresses to record (misaligning GIS links)
    • Misspellings & missing data
    • Duplicate master data
  • Increase deterrence
    • Understand when & where to establish presence
  • Reduce crime, social harm, and crashes
    • Sometimes the answer is to work with public officials
    • Location patterns emerge (and strategy are thus able to be developed)
    • May mainly help reduce property & violent crime
  • Recover uncommitted patrol time
    • Analytics on place, status, and time metrics
  • Increase transparency
    • Automatic reports can facilitate information dissemination
  • Recover budget funding (cost-benefit analysis)
    • DDACTS to reduce crashes; less crime, less cost on patrols
  • Increase arrests

Part 3: Success Stories

Section titled “Part 3: Success Stories”

Crime Intelligence Officer in Longmont, CO led to alley arsonist arrest.

  • Suspect was arrested by sending patrol out to time & place and proved the suspect set nine other fires in the area.

Using DDACTS in Weslaco, TX, an analyst saw overlap of crime & traffic, leading to 147% traffic stops, thereby reducing crime:

  • Crime, Burglary, Theft from Vehicles, etc.

Part 4: Importance of Quality Data

Section titled “Part 4: Importance of Quality Data”

Think in terms of consuming only that data which you’re able to process.

  • Poor quality of data comes from consuming too much data
  • Then also by not properly cleaning & sanitizing that data

What goes into process? Late, poor quality, erroneous data…

  • Results are defined by what is put in to analyze
  • Outcome is a bad cycle of poor quality analysis

Results of Bad Data

Section titled “Results of Bad Data”
  • Under/over counting crime
  • Misidentification of crime patterns
  • Erroneous analysis of crime (including error in forecasting)
  • Incomplete address and person histories
  • Embarrassment in court

Questions

Section titled “Questions”
  1. Data quality is the #1 problem, but limited support by those higher in the Chain of Command.
    • Identify 3 to 5 major issues, then pitch these to Supervisors
    • Does the agency have a data cleaning process or training to do so in place?
  2. I don’t have access and I’m not permitted. (IT Department issue)
    • Maintain the expectation that data is preserved and accessible (between IT & Analysts)
  3. The vendor says it can’t be done, or else costs additional budget. (Vendor issue)
    • Set the expectation that the vendor (should be) paid to provide solutions.
    • Vendor should understand Analysts are utilizing data-driven strategies.
  4. ODBC (Open Database Connection)
    • Microsoft Access can use an ODBC connection to connect directly to the backend database of an RMS, allowing access to live data (rather than downloading it), assuming proper permissions and drivers are configured.
    • Access uses relational databases (using SQL)
