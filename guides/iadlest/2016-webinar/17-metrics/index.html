Improving Metrics in Police Agencies

Christopher Bruce challenges traditional crime statistics, advocating for smarter, context-aware approaches that prioritize actionable insights over arbitrary baselines and category totals. His presentation emphasizes meaningful measurement, accurate categorization, and evidence-based frameworks to better align crime analysis with real-world harm and agency goals.

Improving Metrics in Police Agencies

Christopher Bruce claims to be a working Analyst, not a Statistician. His reports are not based on “thoughtless categories” or “meaningless baselines,” and “meaningless totals.”

  • General statistics have been used for ~100 years.
  • Analyzing crime is really about producing actionable information, rather than churning out reports.
  • Unquantifiable measures of change need to be data points that are requested;
    • Not assumed to be relevant

Better Categorizing

Section titled “Better Categorizing”
Part 1 Crimes Part 1 crimes, also known as index crimes, are a set of serious offenses that are reported to the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) through the Uniform Crime Reporting (UCR) Program.

Part 1 Crimes (UCR) are not useful to Crime Analysts looking to write reports on actionable information.

  • The Part 1 Crime “assault” does not account for the various types of assaults.
  • Conversely, the new reporting system NIBRS enables us to report on more specific crime.

However, because NIBRS permits multiple offenses to be reported per incident, police agencies may need to implement internal policies requiring detailed collection of incident-level data elements.

  • Ensures that reporting reflects actual case facts, enabling data-driven analysis and actionable insights.

Outputs and Outcomes

Section titled “Outputs and Outcomes”
  • Output: Drunk driving arrests
  • Outcome: Alcohol-related crashes

Output = What is produced (e.g., arrests)
Outcome = Actual effect or impact (e.g., crash reduction)

  • More arrests should not be a measurement of total drunk driving:

    • More arrests could mean more drunk drivers, or
    • More resources addressing the issue

    When addressing a particular concern in reports, focus on the effect, rather than the cause.

    • The output is the basis for producing the resulting outcome.
    • However, the outcome is the basis of our Crime report (not the output).

    When the outcome metric is deterred or resolved, the output metric should decrease or be eliminated.

Unquantifiable Data

Section titled “Unquantifiable Data”

Not everything can be quantified in local police metrics.

Examples:

  • Prevalence of drugs
  • Alcohol abuse
  • Human trafficking
  • Terrorism

These are unquantifiable not due to lack of importance, but because police data captures only what’s visible or enforced—not the full scope.

Results of Specific Reporting

Section titled “Results of Specific Reporting”
  • Detailed crime categories = more reliable reports
  • Improper accounting results in:
    • Drastic unexplained increases/decreases
    • More granular crime data
  • External events (natural disasters, sports events) can skew results

When comparing year-on-year:

  • Investigate unusual highs/lows
  • Use average crime rate from prior 5–7 years
  • Account for seasonal changes (economics, politics, etc.)

Better Crime Rates

Section titled “Better Crime Rates”
  • Large visitor populations inflate crime rates (e.g., daytime visitors)
  • Traditional crime rates (based only on residential pop.) are often unfair

Solution:

  • Use daytime population metrics

  • Understand who is actually affected—often daytime population

  • Night vs. day crime should use correct corresponding population metric

  • Use proxies when needed

Proxies

Section titled “Proxies”

When the actual variable isn’t available, proxies help:

  • Municipal employment → city activity
  • City budget → activity
  • Non-fault drivers → traffic density
  • Disabled vehicle calls → traffic volume
  • Parking spaces → real-time usage
  • Drug-related medical calls → drug use
  • Car model year → driver income

Proxies must avoid bias to remain valid.

Is median better than mean?

    • No standard deviation equivalent
    • Harder to express variance

In Summary

Section titled “In Summary”

  • Be strategic: choose metrics that trace crime & disorder

    • Focus on outcomes
    • Don’t measure without knowing why

  • Calculate averages, ranges

    • Use relatable, realistic baselines

  • Index effectively

  • Consider alternatives to population for crime rates

  • Treat demographics like geography
