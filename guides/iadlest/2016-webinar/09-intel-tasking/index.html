Tasking the Collection and Analysis of Intelligence to Inform Decision Making

Intelligence

Section titled “Intelligence”

Intelligence enables us to make informed decisions.

For every potential source, numerous decisions must be made.

  • Human (covert, CHEERS)
  • Technical (overt/covert)
  • OSINT, GEOINT, and more

All surveillance—whether human or technical, overt or covert—requires clear decisions about when, where, and how it is deployed.
Risk must be managed to ensure operational safety.
Analysis will generate new intelligence and highlight gaps requiring relevant, timely information.

National Intelligence Model (UK)

Section titled “National Intelligence Model (UK)”

The National Intelligence Model (NIM) governs how intelligence is managed in the UK.

  • Operates locally, nationally, and internationally
  • All intelligence roles in the UK fall under the NIM
  • Functions through four elements:
  1. People: Defined roles, responsibilities, and analytical skills
  2. Systems: Infrastructure, software, MDTs (e.g., i2, ArcGIS)
  3. Sources: Open/closed, overt/covert, human/technical
  4. Knowledge: Procedural documentation, databases, organizational memory

The Framework of NIM

Section titled “The Framework of NIM”

Think of the NIM framework as a 12-month cycle.

Presented bottom-up (foundation first):

  1. Profiles – Subject, Problem, Community
  2. Tactical Assessments – Bi-weekly or monthly intelligence updates
  3. Strategic Assessments – Long-term plans and control strategies

Intelligence Cycle

Section titled “Intelligence Cycle”

Read: The Intelligence Cycle

  1. Planning & Direction – Identify needs, define goals
  2. Collection – Gather raw intelligence
  3. Processing – Clean, sanitize, and convert for use
  4. Analysis & Production – All-source analysis and interpretation
  5. Dissemination – Timely delivery to decision-makers
  6. Evaluation – Assess effectiveness and feed into the next cycle

Analysts are not an add-on to operations. They are integral to the team.

Conclusion

Section titled “Conclusion”

The structured and methodical approach of the UK’s National Intelligence Model (NIM) demonstrates the vital role of intelligence in supporting strategic and tactical decision-making. Through processes such as tasking, profiling, intelligence collection, and gap analysis, agencies are better equipped to respond to complex criminal threats.

The fictional scenario explored in this document exemplifies how layered intelligence—when guided by a robust framework—can reveal hidden networks, identify key individuals, and inform proactive interventions. The commissioning of a Subject Profile, supported by clear Terms of Reference and a dynamic Collection Plan, illustrates the end-to-end lifecycle of intelligence-led operations.

Ultimately, the value of intelligence is not just in what is known, but in how it is acted upon. From identifying suitable CHIS candidates like Kimberly JONES to forming actionable insights from fragmented data, the intelligence process empowers agencies to protect communities, allocate resources effectively, and disrupt criminal activity with precision.

As analysts and decision-makers work together within this model, they embody the principle that intelligence is information designed for action—transforming data into impact, and uncertainty into clarity.
