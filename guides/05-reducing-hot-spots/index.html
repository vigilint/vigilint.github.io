Reducing Hot Spots

Evidence-based strategies for reducing crime hot spots and solving persistent public safety problems through focused deterrence, environmental design, and data-driven problem analysis.

Strategic Crime Analysis: Reducing Hot Spots & Solving Problems

Successful Problem Solving Efforts

Section titled “Successful Problem Solving Efforts”

Effective, long-term crime-reduction initiatives.

  • Chronic problems can be reduced—often dramatically—with the right focus and strategy.

Focused Deterrence

Section titled “Focused Deterrence”

When consequences are real, meaningful, and swift, behavior change becomes more likely.

The High Point Police Department in North Carolina employs a four-tiered system to categorize domestic violence offenders under its Offender Focused Domestic Violence Initiative (OFDVI):

  • D List – No prior charges, but repeat calls or clear risk factors
  • C List – First domestic violence charge
  • B List – Second DV charge or high-risk behavior; warned directly
  • A List – Chronic, dangerous offenders; subject to immediate arrest

Community-Oriented Policing

Section titled “Community-Oriented Policing”

Through situational crime control and community cooperation, agencies can reduce offenses without relying solely on police presence.

It’s often surprising how basic environmental interventions can reduce the need for officer presence.

Chronic Hot Spots

Section titled “Chronic Hot Spots”

Chronic hot spots vary in severity—from a single residence to entire districts.

  • “Blobs” on hot spot maps identify where problems occur, but not why.
  • Drill down from generalized blobs to specific locations and underlying issues.

Response Models

Section titled “Response Models”
  • Use environmental analysis models and secure community or political buy-in to pursue sustained reductions.
  • Poor environmental design—or failure to maintain it—often attracts criminal activity.

Common Responses

Section titled “Common Responses”

Short-Term

  • Saturation patrols
  • Increased enforcement

Long-Term

  • CPTED (Crime Prevention Through Environmental Design)
  • Place regulation (e.g., zoning, use restrictions)

25 Techniques of Situational Crime Prevention

Section titled “25 Techniques of Situational Crime Prevention”

Crime prevention should address each of five categories:

  1. Increase the effort
  2. Increase the risks
  3. Reduce the rewards
  4. Reduce provocations
  5. Remove excuses

Learn more at the Center for Problem-Oriented Policing.

Problem Analysis, Response, and Assessment

Section titled “Problem Analysis, Response, and Assessment”

A systematic approach leads to tailored responses and measurable outcomes.

Pareto Principle (80/20 Rule)

Section titled “Pareto Principle (80/20 Rule)”
  • Example: 3% of homeless-related addresses account for 26% of CFS.
  • Focus efforts where impact is greatest.

Overrepresentation

Section titled “Overrepresentation”
  • 5 motels with 28% of total rooms may generate 53% of CFS.
  • Reflects power-law distribution and disproportionality in resource demands.

Goldstein Hierarchy of Problems

Section titled “Goldstein Hierarchy of Problems”
  1. Broad Problem Category – e.g., Disorder at motels
  2. General Problem – e.g., High volume of calls at budget motels
  3. Specific Problem Type – e.g., Repeat overdoses at three locations
  4. Particular Problem – e.g., Fentanyl overdoses at the Sunset Motel due to long-term residents and weak guest policies

Why It Matters:

  • Prevents vague definitions
  • Focuses resources
  • Leads to effective, sustainable interventions

Incentives for Operating Permits

Section titled “Incentives for Operating Permits”

Cities can enforce accountability through licensing laws.

In 2006, Chula Vista, CA passed Ordinance No. 3041, requiring all hotels and motels to obtain an annual Permit to Operate. Establishments with recurring crime were required to implement safety improvements to maintain licensure.

  • A blend of analytics + environmental redesign = reduced crime + decreased need for enforcement

Conclusion

Section titled “Conclusion”

Reducing crime hot spots requires more than patrol shifts or enforcement surges—it demands data-driven focus, community engagement, and tailored interventions. Whether addressing a single troubled property or a citywide trend, success comes from understanding the specific problem, selecting appropriate responses, and evaluating impact. Agencies that combine environmental design, focused deterrence, and place-based strategy not only reduce crime but also strengthen public trust and use limited resources more wisely.

Sustained reductions are possible. The key is to move from reacting to patterns toward solving the problems that create them.
